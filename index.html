Executive Summary

Lansing opened in October 2025 as a holiday pop-up and became a year-round store in January 2026. With four months of regular retail data, the picture is mixed but more positive than it first appears. Lansing performs strongly during the holiday quarter and shows excellent customer acquisition signals. Off-season EBITDA is thin and January 2026 was a small loss, but the store is contributing to the business overall.

Headline Takeaway
  • Lansing generated about $125K of EBITDA over its first seven months. Roughly 95% came from the October–December holiday window.
  • Holiday performance was strong. From October to December 2025, sales per square foot ran 15% ahead of Ann Arbor, and Lansing produced about $118K of EBITDA.
  • Off-season results are thin. January through April 2026 produced about $7K of EBITDA total — an average of roughly $1.7K per month. January was a loss at about negative $7K.
  • The off-season gap with Ann Arbor is real. Ann Arbor delivered a 22% EBITDA margin from January through April. Lansing delivered 4%. The main driver is labor productivity, not the product catalog or pricing.
  • Customer signals are excellent. Lansing captures emails on 56% of orders versus 32% at Ann Arbor, and is acquiring new customers at nearly twice the rate.
What This Suggests
  • Lansing is a viable store. It's contributing to the business and the brand is being well received in the market.
  • The off-season operating model is too labor-heavy for current sales volume. This is the most actionable lever.
  • A decision is needed: right-size the cost structure, accept the holiday-concentrated profile as part of the brand strategy, or wait for a full year of data before making changes.

How to Read This Report

Lansing has only been a regular retail store since January 2026. Comparing seven months of Lansing data directly to a mature Ann Arbor store is misleading, because half of Lansing's data comes from a pop-up phase built around holiday demand. To handle this fairly, the report uses three time windows.

  • Full period, October 2025 to April 2026. Useful for overall totals.
  • Pop-up phase, October to December 2025. Lansing operating as a holiday pop-up. This is where its strongest performance shows.
  • Retail phase, January to April 2026. The apples-to-apples view. Both stores in normal retail operation during the same calendar window.

All revenue, gross profit, labor, lease, utilities, marketing, and EBITDA figures come from the store-level P&L. Order counts, AOV, units per transaction, product mix, and customer data come from transaction-level and email capture data. Where the two sources differ on top-line sales, the P&L is the source of truth.

Lansing produced $124,677 of EBITDA over the full seven months. Of that, $118,058 came from October–December and only $6,619 from January–April. January 2026 was a small loss. The off-season is contributing very little after all costs are paid.

Where the Cost Gap Sits

Two Questions Worth Pressure Testing
  • Is Lansing carrying more labor than January–April volume requires? If Lansing matched even 70% of Ann Arbor's labor productivity, retail phase EBITDA would rise from $7K to roughly $26K — about $6.5K per month.
  • Are off-season schedules still sized for the holiday quarter that has now passed? The pop-up phase added staff for the busy season and the store may not have adjusted for the slower months.

What the Numbers Are Saying

Where Lansing Is Winning
  • Holiday quarter EBITDA of $118K shows the store can perform strongly when traffic is high.
  • Sales per square foot during the pop-up phase ran 15% ahead of Ann Arbor.
  • Black Friday weekend at 132% of Ann Arbor's $/sq ft and the December gift-building period at 117% are clear strengths.
  • Email capture rate at 56% of orders is the highest in the comparison and a meaningful brand asset.
  • New customer acquisition is roughly double Ann Arbor's — healthy for a store this young.
  • Total operating expenses as a share of sales are slightly lower than Ann Arbor's. The store isn't operationally bloated.
Where Lansing Is Struggling
  • Off-season EBITDA is thin. January through April averaged about $1.7K per month. January was a loss.
  • Labor consumed 36% of sales in the retail phase, compared to 18% at Ann Arbor. This is the single largest controllable gap.
  • EBITDA margin in the retail phase was 4% at Lansing versus 22% at Ann Arbor.
  • The store is more holiday-concentrated than Ann Arbor. December alone is 37% of seven-month sales, and 95% of period EBITDA came from October–December.

Notes on Method and Caveats

  • Time windows. Three windows are used: the full period (October 2025–April 2026), the pop-up phase (October–December 2025), and the retail phase (January–April 2026). The retail phase is the primary apples-to-apples view.
  • Source of truth. Net sales, gross profit, labor, lease, utilities, marketing, and EBITDA come from the store-level P&L. Order counts, AOV, units per transaction, product mix, and customer data come from transaction-level and email capture data.
  • Gross margin definition. The P&L gross profit figure includes packaging, freight, damages, scrap, and inventory variance in addition to product cost. Pure merchandise margin would be slightly higher. P&L gross margin is used here because it's the audited number that hits net income.
  • Reconciliation note. P&L net sales for Ann Arbor (January–April) is $542,311. Transaction-level net sales for the same window is $543,995. The small gap reflects timing and treatment differences between GL and POS exports and is not material.
  • Square footage. Lansing 2,000 sq ft, Ann Arbor 5,000 sq ft. Sales per square foot is the primary way to normalize for size.
  • Labor productivity. Sales per labor hour uses Hours Worked from the payroll system (excludes PTO and sick time). Labor as a share of sales uses Total Labor Expense from the P&L (includes wages, taxes, benefits, and accruals).
